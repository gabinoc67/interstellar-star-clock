🎐 Interstellar Star Clock System Explanation

📘 Universal 12-Month Mapping

Each planet in our solar system has a unique orbit around the Sun. Instead of using Earth's fixed 365-day calendar, we divide each planet's full orbit into 12 equal segments to represent that planet's own monthly cycle. This helps align familiar month names (January–December) with planetary days or degrees.

🧭 Planetary Clock Fields Explained

Each planetary clock page (e.g., mars_clock.html) includes the following fields:

  • ☿ Mercury Local Date & Time – Current day and sol on Mercury using a 58.65 Earth-day rotation.
  • ♀ Venus Local Date & Time – Tracks Venus' slow retrograde rotation (~243 Earth days per rotation).
  • ♁ Earth CST Dome Time – Constant reference for all planetary domes to keep synced to Earth time, especially for human circadian rhythm.
  • ♂ Mars Local Time – Based on Mars sol (24.6 hours), tracked independently from Earth. Used to locate which Martian month range the current sol belongs to (e.g., 0–57 = January).
  • ♃ Jupiter to 🔷 Pluto – All follow similar logic using orbital period splits and artificial rotation equivalents.
  • 🌌 Interstellar Time – Galactic-age-based timestamp (~57 million+), indicating universal reference time.
  • 🚀 Star Date – Fictional but synchronized stardate system for interstellar logs, anchored to Earth's clock.
  • 📏 Distance from Earth – Live astronomical distance to that planet from Earth (updated dynamically or statically).
  • 🔭 Orbital Position – Planet’s position along its elliptical orbit, in degrees (0°–360°).

🗕 Month Mapping Example for All Planets

Earth: 365 days per year → ~30.4 days/month
Mars: 687 days per year → ~57 days/month
Jupiter: 4380 days per year → ~365 days/month
Pluto: 90,000 days per year → ~7500 days/month

If a clock shows Mars Day 1423, that lands in the equivalent of December (based on Mars month 633–687).
