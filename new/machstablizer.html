Program Purpose

This simulator is a theoretical Mach 10 aircraft stability model. It does not create anti-gravity. The sphere is modeled as a belly-mounted inertial equilibrium stabilizer that helps keep the craft centered in a planned atmospheric corridor.

The key idea is line-of-sight control: Ground Spot A, Ground Spot B, and Aircraft C form a triangle. The aircraft compares its real altitude and location against the planned altitude corridor, then makes small corrections.

Sphere Stabilizer Algorithm

The sphere does not stop the wind. The nose still cuts through the airflow. The belly sphere only stabilizes the aircraft by correcting force imbalance.

Ceq = w1ΔC² + w2Δg² + w3ΔP² + w4ΔT² + w5Δq²
Sphere Output = Kp(error) + Kd(dError/dt) + Gyro Damping
G Smooth = 100 − |Lift/Weight − 1| × Gain

Line-of-Sight Algorithm

Two ground locations form a reference base. Aircraft C must remain on the altitude level between them.

RangeA = Distance(Ground A, Aircraft C)
RangeB = Distance(Ground B, Aircraft C)
θA = atan(h / RangeA)
θB = atan(h / RangeB)
Altitude Error = hplanned − hmeasured

Important Disclaimer

This webpage is a conceptual simulator. It is not CFD, not a certified aircraft controller, not a real Mach 10 design, and not a proof of artificial gravity. It is a logic model for reference-based inertial stability.

The phrase “sphere gravity stabilizer” is used here as a theory name. In engineering terms, it means a spherical inertial equilibrium stabilizer.

Expanded Version 2 Goal: Pre-calculate a Point A to Point B Mach 10 atmospheric corridor, lock the aircraft between two ground reference spots, track the altitude level by line-of-sight angles, stabilize belly force with a sphere core, and sequence cooling/material protection by clock-controlled sensor readings.
