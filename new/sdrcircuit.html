SDR Circuit Equation Simulator

Source, Deviation, and Return conceptual simulator based on the SDR Equation. This model visualizes identity, deviation, approximation, and recognition as a symbolic closed-loop system.

Simulation Intention

This simulator is not presented as a proof of physical law. Its purpose is to make the SDR paper more understandable by converting the author’s mathematical terminology into observable symbolic behavior. The simulation shows how a system begins at identity, moves through deviation, approaches the golden-ratio approximation, and then returns to exact identity through recognition/normalization.

Core thesis displayed by the simulator: Addition can recover φ, but division restores 1.

Source

1 × 1 = 1

The source state is complete self-identity. In the simulator, this is the stable reference value.

Deviation

1 + n, 1 − n, 0 − 1

Deviation means the system leaves the reference state through excess, reduction, or inversion.

Approximation

φ = 1.61803398875

φ represents the organized ceiling of additive growth. It is structured, but it is not identical to 1.

Return

n ÷ n = 1

Division is treated as recognition or normalization. The system measures itself by itself and returns to identity.

Realistic Interpretation

To make the author’s document stronger, this simulator should be described as a conceptual systems model rather than a verified physical theory. In realistic engineering language, the value 1 acts like a reference state, deviation acts like system error, φ acts like an optimized but non-identical approximation, and division acts like normalization or recalibration.

This makes the model comparable to feedback stabilization, error correction, recursive optimization, and closed-loop control systems. The simulator helps readers understand the theory’s internal logic without claiming that the symbolic operators are experimentally proven forces of nature.

Scientific and Mathematical Disclaimer: This simulator is a symbolic interpretation of the SDR Equation. Standard mathematics confirms identities such as 1 × 1 = 1 and n ÷ n = 1 for nonzero n, but the meanings of “source,” “deviation,” “recognition,” and “return” are philosophical interpretations applied by the framework. The simulator is intended for visualization, education, theory development, and conceptual analysis.
