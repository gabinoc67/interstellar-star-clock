Explanation Panel

An axiom is a starting principle the model assumes before derivation begins. In this page, the axioms are not treated as confirmed physical laws. They are treated as theoretical foundations that must still be translated into equations, observables, and experiments. The code’s annihilating-return stability test is not mystical language. It is simply the program’s way of asking whether a system can cycle back into a coherent state without flattening into zero structure or flying apart into instability. If the score stays high, the system maintains repeated identity. If it falls low, the system loses persistence. This is why the center pulse, clock drift, and stability bars matter: they turn philosophical claims into visible, inspectable behavior.
Using atomic clocks, CST clocks, and interstellar clocks helps because clocks are measurable. If projection tension really changes geometry, then it should influence timekeeping under known gravitational conditions. That gives the theory a path from abstract ideas to potential experiments. To strengthen the theory further, one would need a direct mapping from the axioms into a metric theory with clear predictions that match or outperform existing relativity tests.

Research Improvement Goal

This program is trying to improve the theory by forcing it into a layered stack: ontology, dynamics, geometry, and measurement. The simulator asks whether the axioms can generate persistent structure, whether that structure can remain stable across repeated cycles, and whether its geometry-like behavior can be translated into clock drift and projection tension observables. That is useful because a theory becomes stronger when its assumptions can fail under explicit tests.
