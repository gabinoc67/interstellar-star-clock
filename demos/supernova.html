What this Simulation Does

This simulator models how light from a distant supernova is detected on Earth, explicitly showing:
  • The Sun-centered (heliocentric) geometry
  • The Earth’s orbital position around the Sun
  • The observer’s latitude and longitude on Earth
  • A photon traveling from the supernova to Earth (visual animation)
  • How redshift, distance, Heff, φ, and CST-adjusted φ are computed
  • Light-curve timing markers and cosmological time dilation: Δtobs = (1+z)·Δtrest
  • Distance-mode switch: DL vs DC vs DA to show how (1+z) packaging bends φ(z)
  • Optional cosmology calculator mode for DL and lookback time
Note: The “two-marker” feature measures time stretch and light-curve structure, not changes in the speed of light (c is constant).

Equations Used (Paper-style + CST + Time Dilation)

Distance modulus: μ = 5·log10(DL/Mpc) + 25 ⇒ DL = 10(μ − 25)/5 Mpc (observational definition)
Distance mode switch: DL = (1+z)·DC | DC = DL/(1+z) | DA = DL/(1+z)2
Heff(z) = (c · z) / Dmode(z)
φ(z) = arctan(Heff / H0,ref)
Toy observer redshift: zobs = z + (v⊕/c) · cos(lat) · cos(lon)
Cosmological time dilation: Δtobs = (1+z)·Δtrest
φCST = φ · CST
Flat ΛCDM: DL=(1+z)·(c/H0)·∫0→z dz′/E(z′), E(z)=√(Ωm(1+z)3+ΩΛ)
Lookback: tL=(1/H0)·∫0→z dz′/((1+z′)E(z′))
What the Distance Mode Toggle Proves (the (1+z) test):
If you switch from DL to DC=DL/(1+z), then Heff increases by (1+z):
H_eff,C = (1+z)·H_eff,L
So φ(z) “lifts up” and the downward bend past z≈0.1 flattens a lot if the bend was mainly the (1+z) wrapper. It will not be perfectly flat if the underlying distance (or integral) still encodes real H(z) evolution.

Supernova Physics Context (why supernovae matter)

Supernovae can eject several solar masses at speeds reaching a few percent of the speed of light, driving an expanding shock wave into the interstellar medium and creating a supernova remnant. They enrich the galaxy with heavy elements (from oxygen through heavier nuclei) and their shocks can trigger new star formation. They also contribute to cosmic rays and, in some cases, may be associated with gravitational-wave sources (depending on the progenitor and explosion dynamics).

The Connection (1998 Type Ia → Dark Energy):
By comparing Type Ia supernova brightness vs redshift, teams in the late 1990s found distant SNe Ia were fainter than expected in a decelerating universe—interpreted as evidence the cosmic expansion is accelerating, leading to “dark energy.” This simulator’s distance-mode switch helps separate “distance-definition curvature” (like (1+z) packaging) from genuine H(z) evolution.
